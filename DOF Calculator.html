Circle of Confusion Multiplier

The CoC (circle of confusion) is the maximum blur diameter that still appears acceptably sharp. This tool derives it from your sensor's pixel pitch rather than the old print-based approximation.

1× (pixel-level) — Airy disk = 1 pixel. Sharpness judged at 100% zoom / maximum resolution. Very demanding; only achievable at wide apertures on high-MP sensors.

2× (default) — Accounts for Bayer/X-Trans demosaic interpolation smearing across ~2 pixels. A good standard for critical work and large prints.

5–6× — Equivalent to viewing a print at normal distance. Matches what most DoF apps show for APS-C sensors.

~10× (classic) — Traditional print-based standard (sensor diagonal ÷ 1500). Matches legacy DoF tables and apps like Photo Pills or Classic DoF. Hyperfocal distances will be much shorter.

Increasing the multiplier makes the CoC larger → shallower hyperfocal, wider DoF, higher diffraction limit.

Lens & Focus

Focal length — the lens focal length in mm. Longer focal lengths compress depth of field; wider lenses have much greater DoF at the same aperture and distance.

Aperture — the f-number. Stopping down (larger f-number) increases DoF but also increases diffraction softening. The slider turns orange/red as you approach or exceed the diffraction limit for your current CoC.

Focus distance — where the lens is focused. DoF is asymmetric: there is always more depth behind the focus point than in front, increasingly so at longer distances.

DoF scales with the square of focal length and linearly with aperture and CoC. Halving the focal length quadruples the DoF.

Hyperfocal Distance

The hyperfocal distance (H) is the closest focusing distance at which everything from H/2 to infinity is acceptably sharp. It is the most DoF you can get from a given lens, aperture, and CoC.

Formula: H = f² / (N × CoC) where f is focal length and N is f-number.

Practical use: For landscape and street photography, focusing at the hyperfocal distance maximises the range of sharp subject matter. At pixel-accurate CoC (2×) the hyperfocal is much further than traditional tables suggest — high-MP cameras demand more precise focus.

H/2 rule: When focused at the hyperfocal, the near limit of sharpness is exactly half the hyperfocal distance.

The hyperfocal distance shown here uses your current CoC setting. Increasing the multiplier shortens the hyperfocal significantly.

Field of Sharpness — Side View

A scaled top-down view of the scene, showing the camera at left and the depth zones extending to the right.

Amber zone — nearer than the DoF near limit: out of focus in front of the subject.

Teal zone — the acceptably sharp region between the near and far DoF limits. The width of this zone is the total depth of field.

Purple zone — beyond the DoF far limit: out of focus behind the subject.

Focus point (●) — where the lens is actually focused. Maximum sharpness is here; DoF extends symmetrically only at close distances, and asymmetrically (more behind) at longer ones.

H marker — the hyperfocal distance, shown when it falls within the visible scene range.

When DoF is very thin the diagram zooms in automatically and shows a ◀ cam indicator — the camera is off-canvas to the left.

Aperture vs Effective Sharpness

Shows how two competing factors change as you stop down, helping you find the aperture sweet spot for your sensor and current focus distance.

Teal curve (DoF) — depth of field grows as you stop down. The curve rises steeply at wide apertures and flattens as you approach the hyperfocal distance.

Red curve (Diffraction) — the Airy disk diameter grows linearly with f-number. Once it exceeds your CoC (the red dashed line), diffraction softening reduces resolution faster than stopping down gains DoF.

Blue line — your current aperture. Move the aperture slider and watch it shift.

Red dashed line — the diffraction limit: Airy disk = CoC. The red background zone to its right is the diffraction-degraded region.

The sweet spot is just before the red dashed line — maximum DoF with minimum diffraction penalty. Adjusting the CoC multiplier moves the diffraction limit left or right.
